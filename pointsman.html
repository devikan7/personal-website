The challenge

The newsletter had built a loyal daily readership, but growth was linear — relying entirely on organic discovery and social sharing. There was no systematic way to reward the most engaged readers or incentivize them to bring in new subscribers.

The question was: how do you turn an existing base of passionate readers into a growth engine — without paid ads, without spammy tactics, and without breaking the trust that made the community special?

My role & approach

As Head of Product, I conceived, designed, and shipped Pointsman end-to-end — from the growth strategy and scoring algorithm through the full-stack implementation and launch campaign. The goal was to build a self-reinforcing flywheel: readers earn points for engagement, share to earn more, and the leaderboard creates competitive energy that drives viral referrals.

The system was intentionally built to be lightweight and fast — a Next.js app backed by Redis for real-time point calculations, with cron jobs processing subscriber engagement data in batches overnight so the dashboard was always fresh by morning.

The scoring engine

The algorithm was designed to be transparent and fair — rewarding both loyalty (reading consistently) and growth (referring new subscribers). Every reader starts with a base of 100 points, then earns incrementally for every email opened and every share that converts.

Points Algorithm
100 = Base points for every reader
+ Emails read × 2 points each
+ Referral shares × 5 points each
Total Points = 100 + (emails_read × 2) + (shares × 5)

The 5x multiplier on shares was the key insight — it made referring a friend worth more than reading 2.5 emails, creating a clear incentive to share. Combined with a percentile-based ranking system, readers could see exactly where they stood versus the community, fueling competitive engagement.

How I built it

1

Identified the growth lever

Analyzed engagement data and discovered a power-user segment — thousands of subscribers who opened every single email, every morning. They were the most likely to refer if given the right incentive and recognition.

2

Designed the points algorithm

Created a scoring model that weighted shares at 5x vs. reads at 2x, making referrals the highest-leverage action. Added percentile rankings so readers could see their relative standing in the community.

3

Built the full-stack product

Shipped a Next.js web app with Redis-backed real-time points lookup, cron-based batch processing for subscriber data, Airtable for verified user management, and a leaderboard with masked emails for privacy.

4

Launched with a viral loop

Integrated "Tweet This" and social sharing directly into the points dashboard. Readers shared their scores, which drove curiosity and new signups — 10,000 people signed up and referred on day one.

Product pillars

📈

Engagement Scoring

Points algorithm that tracked emails read, open streaks, and referral shares — giving readers a tangible measure of their loyalty and a reason to keep coming back.

🏆

Live Leaderboard

Real-time ranking system showing top readers with verified badges and masked emails for privacy. Created healthy competition and social proof for the community.

🚀

Viral Sharing Mechanics

One-tap social sharing built into the points dashboard. Pre-composed tweets with personalized scores made sharing effortless and drove organic viral loops.

⚡

Real-Time Infrastructure

Redis-backed points lookup for instant results, cron-based batch processing for overnight data sync, and a lightweight Next.js frontend for fast, responsive UX.

Key learnings

The biggest lesson from Pointsman was that product-led growth doesn't need to be complex — it needs to be psychologically right. The leaderboard, the percentile ranking ("top 3% of readers"), and the one-click tweet were more powerful than any referral bonus or discount could have been.

The second insight was about speed. The entire product was designed, built, and launched in under two weeks. By keeping the architecture simple — a serverless Next.js app, Redis for state, and cron for batch processing — we avoided over-engineering and shipped something that worked at scale from day one.
